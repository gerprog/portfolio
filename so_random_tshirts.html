So Random T-Shirts

So Random T-Shirts is a solo college project built with Ruby on Rails. The idea is that a one off T-Shirt will be easily generated randomly with various image and text.

A user firstly selects from a grid of random small images, then sees a T-Shirt which has text and image randomly selected. They can try again until an interesting juxtaposition is arrived at. A user can comment, share and upload images in the forum.

Technology used:

HTML5, CSS3, AdobeCS
Ruby on Rails
JavaScript
JQueryUI
SQLite3
Disqus provides the forum.

So Random T-Shirts is an e-commerce web application that allows the user to generate their own product to buy. The random selection of a single colour image and single colour text creates interesting juxtapositions. Control is surrendered over what image and text the t-shirt will have but for the option of ‘Try Again’. A wide variety of texts are within the database so there is no telling what the result will be. An option is available to alter default text colours when buying.

Development

The main requirements were:
• that the app is simple, fun, slightly addictive, interactive.
• it keeps the user interested enough so they become a customer and buy personalised T-shirts.
• that there is easy management of orders and users for admin.

The initial idea was playing on a user’s interest in seeing their ‘fortune’. Then perhaps liking their fortune enough to have printed as a t-shirt. This was deemed to have a very limited market as the vast majority do not think the resulting text would relate to themselves, and if they did are not very likely to want to display it publicly.

Expanding on that initial concept, the idea of generating a random message, quote, or joke with a random image behind was deemed to have a greater appeal. With each result being unique.

Testing

How would a user react to being asked to attempt at making a custom T-Shirt whose outcome was not fully within their control? Each of the four users tested found the random mixing of image and texts to be interesting and fun.

The first iteration of the home page had the navigation show a range of different types of image to choose from for display on the grid. Pets, Colours, People, Places, Toys etc. as well as the option for all images at once. Initial tests with this setup resulted in all users ignoring the options except for the default ‘All’ grid, so they were removed and there is just one grid to choose from.

This grid is randomly shuffled each time the page is viewed, a user selects images they like which are added to a row bottom of screen. Once full, the row reveals ‘Click here to see your T-Shirt!’. A t-shirt’s large coloured text over an image is random and the results will be different each time. Resulting combinations lead to interesting interpretations of meaning and design. A user can register and share their t-shirt in the forum. They can also buy it as a one off.

An early prototype showed that users are unlikely to want to repeat the exact same process of picking 7 images for the ‘row’ just to generate a new t-shirt. So once in the ‘Shop’, there is the option of pressing ‘Try Again’ to keep generating new image and text combinations. A couple lines of text over the nav bar explain to users they may use ‘start’ and see what happens or they may ‘cheat’ and head directly to the shop.

Feedback indicated that the cart needed to always display not only on the left hand side which is showing the current total price, but also as its own page where full details and images can be seen.

Users wanted to have a large and varied sample of potential t-shirts be clearly available at all times, so a gallery was introduced. Sample t-shirts moving on the sides are a reminder of the possibilities available, also the sign up and log in pages show more possible t-shirts.

Users wanted the buttons to be as big as possible.

For admin, the orders show customer shipping details, total paid, payment type, text colour chosen, along with a small image of the T-Shirts bought.

In retrospect the grid element could be seen as useless. It is not generating the t-shirt design, and users might be confused trying to establish connection with the grid and a t-shirt.
