Crazy Creatures

Crazy Creatures is a lively and fun game for young children. The goal is to create creatures by answering questions and doing tasks.

This is a college project with a team of 3.

The quizzes and jigsaw encourage constructive learning. The animals could be replaced by geographic or interstellar maps, body parts etc.

The final stage is a bonus where the player can randomly select and create new creatures in full or simply have fun with some unexpected results.

Sound on is best, if you dare!

I created the creatures using Adobe Photoshop.

Technology used:

HTML5, CSS3, Adobe CS.
JQueryUI for the drag and drop functionality.
JavaScript and JSON for the quiz elements.

Game Design

A short and simple game for young children, ages 4 to 6. There are five stages and then a bonus. The prime objective is helping children develop their natural love of animals through constructive learning, a game of recognition, and maybe learning an animal’s name and even how it is spelt!

Along the way there are questions from various categories, some of which are related to animals. Some of the questions may be a challenge to the very young but along with adult help a solution can be explained. If playing by themselves, simple elimination will guide a child to the correct answer and a new fact or word may be discovered.

There is a lot of colour, sound and movement to keep the player’s attention. Each stage is distinct from the others, creatures on the sides dance up and down, more creatures appear on the sides in a game of 'catch me if you can'. Palm trees appear to grow and sway to the jungle drums as the game progresses.

As the stages are completed the creatures bounce and make a sound as if coming to life. While enhancing the effect of ‘making’ a creature this also creates a sense of character for each.

The initial idea was to create an interactive quiz for children based on the idea of rotating cubes.

At the bottom there are seven question marks, these ‘badges’ will be filled with the creatures as we play. A shuffle movement on the badges at the start of each stage gives a sense of personality to the creatures and encourages the player to fill them all. A child will enjoy seeing their collection grow, giving a sense of progress.

A sense of creating a small world is important, an environment the child could enjoy and want to experience again.

In the final stage the big buttons randomly bring up a head, a torso, or feet. The fun sounds, and unexpected resulting combinations should be amusing for children. The creatures here are new too. A child may try to complete a creature in full, or just enjoy the resulting oddities.

Development

For the first 3 stages based on multiple choice, once the team had an image displaying with a correct answer to a question, we moved into developing a scoring system that would allow us to:
• Display the score on-screen
• Display the next question
• Display the appropriate image
• Display the ‘next stage button’

Once the score gave us the basis of the functionality we moved on to link the different stages of the game.

As we progressed we tried to enhance the user experience by adding music, animated features, and bright background colour.

Care was taken to ensure adherence to Nielsen’s 10 Usabililty Heuristics.

Testing

When the basics were in place the application was tested with a small group of children aged 4 to 8. This proved to be very beneficial towards refining the UX.

The feedback highlighted:
• The 60 seconds timer for questions seemed appropriate for the children to complete the task in hand, but much less was needed to guess an animal.
• The need for background music.
• After playing a number of rounds, the eldest child found that the questions and answers were always displayed in the same order, making the game too predictable.
• That it should be possible to go back to replay a stage.
• That the multiple choice answers were very funny.

Children seemed to especially like the sounds and the creatures moving/hiding to the sides of the game’s container. All levels were passed with different levels of ease. The older child particularly enjoyed finding the right answer to questions in stages 1 to 3.

The younger children preferred to play with the jigsaw style of stage 5, or the bonus, and found the latter part of the game more entertaining, whereas the eldest enjoyed the whole game equally.

The older child thought the game needed more levels, although it was understood it was designed with younger children in mind.

After reviewing this feedback the team implemented a number of changes such as:
• Answer randomization so they appear different with each play.
• Background music.
• Personalization (via cookies).
• Once the game is finished the user can choose to replay entirely or select a specific stage.

The game provides a general knowledge quiz for the young learner. For further implementation there would be the possibility to introduce specific learning topics. The jigsaw stage could be modified to display a map, human body parts, or other components. The game could also act as an aid for children with learning difficulties. Today assistive technologies are helping children with poor reading or writing skills.
